---
title: 'Why Batticaloa Cashews Are Different'
date: '2024-04-11'
---

Ask anyone from the Eastern Province what food defines Batticaloa, and **cashews** will come up within seconds. The region's red laterite soil and dry climate create growing conditions that produce a cashew with a distinctly **sweeter, fuller flavour** compared to cashews grown elsewhere in Sri Lanka.

Cashew season runs roughly from **February to April**, and during this time the landscape transforms. Trees heavy with the unusual cashew apple — a swollen, juicy fruit that the nut hangs beneath — line the roads heading out of town.

The cashew apple itself is just as interesting as the nut:

- It is technically the **false fruit** — the true fruit is the nut
- It ferments quickly and is used to make a local toddy
- Fresh cashew apple juice is tart, astringent, and refreshing on a hot day

Processing the raw nut is skilled work. The shell contains **caustic oil** that burns skin, so roasting and shelling is done carefully by hand in small operations across the district.

Batticaloa cashews rarely make it far — most are sold locally or carried back by visitors as gifts. If you ever find a bag labelled *Batti cashews*, do not hesitate.
