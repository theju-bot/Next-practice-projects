---
title: 'The Kovils of Batticaloa and the Culture They Carry'
date: '2024-05-18'
---

Scattered across Batticaloa and its surrounding villages are **Hindu kovils** — temples that are as much community anchors as they are places of worship. Many of them are centuries old, built and rebuilt across generations of Tamil families who settled the eastern coast long before colonial arrival.

The most significant annual event tied to these kovils is the **Thiruchendur festival season**, when streets that are otherwise quiet fill with:

- Elaborate *kavadi* processions where devotees carry decorated arches as acts of devotion
- The sharp, rhythmic sound of *nadaswaram* and *thavil* echoing through the night
- Oil lamps lit in long rows outside homes to welcome the procession

Each kovil has its own calendar, its own presiding deity, and its own community of families responsible for its upkeep. The role of temple caretaker — *poosari* — is often hereditary, passing from father to son across many generations.

What strikes visitors most is how **inseparable** temple life is from daily life here. The kovil is not a place people go to occasionally. It marks births, deaths, harvests, and new years. It holds the memory of a community that has survived displacement, conflict, and rebuilding — and is still standing.
